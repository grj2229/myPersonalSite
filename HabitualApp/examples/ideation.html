Scenarios

Scenario 1

Jennifer is a college student in her second year. She has been living on campus for a few months during the pandemic and was previously at home with her family taking remote classes. When she lived at home, she had free time to start new routines and hobbies that would benefit her physical health, mental health, and sense of enjoyment. Some of these activities included exercising outside, learning healthy recipes, and connecting with friends virtually. However, she got to a point of feeling very unmotivated and unfocused, and she was never able to continue these routines for an extended period of time. Now, she is busy at school but still feels unmotivated and doesn’t feel a sense of structure in her life. Jennifer downloads an app called Habitual and learns more about how she can create her own routine. Her favorite routine that she began at home was learning and cooking two new healthy recipes each week. Since she lives in an apartment with a kitchen, she decides she wants to pick up this cooking routine again. She pulls out a notebook and writes out all the steps, which include researching recipes, buying the ingredients, and setting aside time each week to prepare the meals. Then, she builds her routine on the app and adds features like the description, category, materials needed, and images associated with each step. She also decides how long she wants to continue this routine for, and then she submits the routine and shares it with her friend.

Scenario 2

Madeline is an incoming first-year undergraduate student. After graduating high school, she has a lot of time for herself while staying at home during the pandemic. She became lazy and feels like she has no reason to do anything. Lying around all day began to take a toll on her mental health, so she wants to implement some healthy routines. She’s not sure where to start so she navigates to the Habitual app’s existing routines page. She scrolls through the featured routines and starts the “Couch to 5k” routine. Madeline also wants to pick up a new hobby, so she goes back to the existing routines page to scroll through the categories for inspiration. As a person of Asian descent, the category for the Asian tag catches her eye. She sees a routine called “DIY Boba” that teaches a new boba flavor once a week. Because buying boba is expensive, Madeline decides to try out the “DIY Boba” routine for fun. Madeline has to first buy the necessary materials, so she saves the “DIY Boba” routine so that she can access and start it later when she has all the ingredients.

Scenario 3

Jill is a second year student at Blonde University. She is currently taking a semester off and living at home because of the pandemic. While she’s staying at home, she made it a goal to focus on activities she’s interested in or didn’t have time to pursue while she was at school. Some of those activities include baking, working out, journaling and working at a coffee shop. She found this app called Habitual that helped her set up her routine for the week as well as show her other hobbies she may pick up. She started off strong and began working towards her goal with the app for the first week, however, for the past 2 days, it’s been a challenge for Jill to get out of her bed and continue with her tasks. She gets gentle reminders with notifications about her goals and how long it’s been since she last opened the app. She finally decides to open it and is greeted with a sad tree that needs her help to stay alive. The more often she goes through building her routine and checking off the tasks she completes, the healthier and stronger her tree becomes but because of the lack of usage for the app for the past 2 days, the tree has been wilting and sad. She can use her points/ coins she collected from completing all her tasks from the previous week to help her tree stay strong. Jill was happy that she was able to both save her tree and some time to relax in bed, and will start back up with her tasks tomorrow.





Design Directions



Design 1:

Strengths
  • Productivity-focused
  • Grid-like structure for easy navigation
  • Easy to find routines and tasks for each day
Weaknesses
  • Doesn’t feel as personal
  • Tree doesn’t visually match with rest of design
  • Home page looks quite similar to other apps on the market


Design 2:

Strengths
  • Personable
  • Uses everyday language
  • Progress bar easy to interpret
  • Personalized feed page
Weaknesses
  • Not as easy to find button to create a new routine
  • Dashboard isn’t as consistent with design
  • First and second screens have very similar layouts




Takeaways from Review Session


From our review session, our classmates liked the layout of the pages and the low stakes our app provides to the users in completing their tasks. Some feedback from our peers is thinking about how to make our app unique and stand out from other scheduling apps. Another suggestion we got is how to get users to start using the app and stay motivated to continue using it as well. One big suggestion we took into consideration was how to make this app personable and make sure there aren't factors that demotivate our users (ex. dying tree idea). We knew that if it's hard for users to start a routine, adding a factor that can potentially demotivate the user wouldn't be best for our design. One way we thought about tackling that issue is with a buddy system or way for users to connect with friends/ family to receive encouraging messages and hold themselves accountable.
